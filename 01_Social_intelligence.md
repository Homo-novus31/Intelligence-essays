The human brain has not significantly evolved over 100,000 years, and yet in the course of this time we discovered agriculture and natural sciences, built civilisations, landed on the surface of the Moon and then went on to discover Artificial intelligence, but a question arises.

When we haven’t gotten more “intelligent”, how are we capable of such wonder?

The answer doesn’t lie in the structure of the brain itself as early research in the 1900s suggested but in the various interactions we have with each other-by sharing ideas and arguments. Through these interactions, intelligence is not expressed; but distributed, amplified and continually evolving. This exact idea was first brought to light by Prof. Dunbar in his Social Brain Hypothesis.

Here, social interaction refers not merely to casual communication but to structured coordination between individuals within societies, organisations, or collaborative environments.
Fast forward three decades, and here we are in a race to build superintelligent AI, pouring trillions of dollars into scaling it, optimising for individual problem-solving instead of a complex social interaction, which is ironic.

When our intelligence arose from social interactions why are we building systems in isolation?

Humans have been social creatures from the dawn of time and have modified their environment to aid their evolutionary hallmark. Current Agentic AI Systems work on reward functions and optimisation, clearly lacking the judgment of actions, neither having knowledge about the context nor the social consequences and fails to model the real world where there are unimaginably large, dense interactions within an organisation or a community and hence will invariably struggle no matter the amount of “intelligence” it may be scaled to, struggling in basic tasks when put into the real world without a “master” i,e. Us, holding it’s reins and commanding it for every single step even though it was meant to automate our task.




Social-AI:- The next big thing 

Social-AI can achieve greater efficiency and superior outcomes by shifting from individual model scaling to collaborative, and amplified intelligent competency. Recent research on Social-AI aims to represent this shift in the development of AI.

For instance, emerging frameworks like Adaptive Theory of Mind (A-ToM) where agents improve outcomes by estimating a partner’s reasoning depth (be it a human or a bot!) in real-time and selecting actions that structurally align with it, adding to isolated agents’ cooperative capabilities combined with shared social memory, a computational mechanism which enable agents to store and recall social knowledge (past interactions and context), these approaches help agents learn in dynamic exchanges. More broadly, recent benchmarks such as SOCIALIQA and ToMI, along with dynamic, multi-agent environments like SOTOPIA and CAMEL highlight the growing importance of social reasoning and interaction in achieving better performance in dynamic settings. Together, these developments point toward a shift from isolated problem solving to coordinated interacting “intelligence”

Real-world implications of Social-AI

This shift is not just in letter but directly affects how future AI Systems will function in domains’ which demand coordination, negotiation and shared decision making, especially in Healthcare, Education and Logistics: what changes is not just performance, but the structure of these systems’ themselves.

Social-AI in Healthcare:-

Current Healthcare system lacks complete coordination between the doctors, records of patients and pharmacists; AI being non-existent in majority of this equation but with Social-AI, agents can seamlessly coordinate across roles (diagnosis agent-treatment planner-patient monitor), alongside doctors and pharmacists transforming Healthcare into a coordinated intelligent system.

Social-AI in Education:-

Students use AI tutors from completing their homework to prepping for exams; Current AI tutors being largely generalised fail to understand the individual needs of each student due to which a wedge is created between AI tutors and teachers (Well whom to follow because each one gives a different way to learn the exact same concept?), Social-AI can possibly bridge this gap by interacting with teachers all the while giving students a personalised and a dynamic learning environment instead of a static and passive one, bringing life to the otherwise stale field of current Education.

Social-AI in Logistics:-

Logistics is one of those fields which has that constant disorientation because human needs are so chaotic; a product in one place may run out faster than the other for example, current logistics have a great problem:-Predicting the shifting consumer preferences and emotional responses. Social-AI can analyse consumer sentiment on different products and coordinate alongside logistic teams to help create an optimised route for transport (No shipping delays) suiting our needs better!

Making Logistics a “living and breathing” optimised system.

But can we truly build Social-AI in an age where people remain apprehensive about the growing influence of Artificial Intelligence across all domains?

I believe the answer doesn’t depend on our readiness, but on our ability to co-exist, adapt and take responsibility for how these systems evolve. What lies ahead is not just the next step in technology but an opportunity to reshape the way we build and operate systems, one that is through productive interactions, not isolation.
